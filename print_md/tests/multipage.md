## Part One

The convoy left before dawn. Fourteen vehicles, two of them armoured, none of them quiet. The road south had been cleared the previous afternoon but clearance in this part of the country meant something specific and temporary — passable, not safe.

Yusra rode in the third truck, between the medic's kit and a crate of water purification tablets. She had not slept. She had not told anyone she had not slept.

"How far to the checkpoint?" she asked.

"Four hours in good conditions," said the driver, whose name she had already forgotten. "Six if the bridge is out again."

She looked at her watch. She looked out the window. The sky was doing something complicated behind the hills — not quite light, not quite dark, a negotiation between states she recognised from other early mornings in other countries where she had been awake when she shouldn't have been.

---

## Part Two

The checkpoint was manned by four soldiers and a dog that had no interest in them. The soldiers had a great deal of interest, which made the stop longer than it needed to be.

Yusra handed over the documents three times. Each time they were studied with an attention that felt theatrical. She had learned not to say anything during this part. The saying-nothing was its own kind of performance, and she had gotten good at it.

The medic, whose name was Okon, stood beside her and breathed slowly through his nose. She found this calming. She suspected he did it on purpose.

"This one," said the soldier, pointing at a name on the manifest.

"That's me," said Yusra.

He looked at her for a long time. Then he handed back the documents and waved them through.

---

## Part Three

The road on the other side of the checkpoint was worse. Not impassably worse, but worse in the way that communicated intent — this is not a road for you, this road is for people who have no choice.

They had no choice, so they drove it.

Okon fell asleep somewhere in the second hour. His head tipped against the window and he breathed with the same deliberate evenness he used when awake, which Yusra found mildly enviable. She watched the landscape shift from scrub to something harder, more decided.

By mid-morning they could see the camp.

---

## Part Four

The camp was larger than the briefing had suggested. This was always the case. The briefings were compiled from satellite images and secondhand reports and the specific optimism of people who were not going to be there. The reality of a place was always larger, louder, more particular.

Yusra climbed out of the truck and stood in the heat and tried to see all of it at once, which was not possible.

A woman was walking toward them with a clipboard and an expression that communicated welcome and exhaustion in equal measure.

"You're late," she said.

"The bridge," said Yusra.

The woman nodded. "The bridge," she agreed, as though this explained everything, because it did.

---

## Part Five

They unloaded for four hours. The water tablets first, then the medical supplies, then the things that were harder to categorise — equipment for the field kitchen, a generator part, six boxes labelled in a language Yusra didn't read.

Okon sorted and logged. The driver helped without being asked. Yusra coordinated and tried not to look like she was coordinating, because the people who lived here had been coordinating longer and better than she had and didn't need to be reminded that she was new to their particular situation even if she was not new to situations.

By the time the trucks were empty the sun had moved into a different quarter of the sky and her shirt was soaked through.

"Good work," said the woman with the clipboard. Her name was Fatou. Yusra had learned it in the second hour.

"Yours was harder," said Yusra.

Fatou looked at her steadily. "Yes," she said. "It was."
